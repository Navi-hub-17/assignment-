Birds

AMERICAN ROBIN

The American robin, a migratory bird, is of the true thrush genus, Named after the European robin for its reddish-orange breast, Though not closely related, it shares its name. Widely distributed throughout North America, It's the most abundant landbird in the region. Active during the day, it gathers in large flocks at night, Feeding on invertebrates, fruits, and berries, it nests early and sings at dawn.

MOURNING DOVE

The mourning dove, a member of the dove family Columbidae, Also known as the American mourning dove and by various other names, Is one of the most abundant and widespread North American birds. Popular as a gamebird, millions are shot annually in the U.S. for sport and meat. Its prolific breeding enables it to sustain its population under hunting pressure. Capable of producing up to six broods of two young each in warm areas annually. Known for its whistling wings upon take-off and landing, a form of sonation. Light gray and brown in color, both males and females look similar.

EUROPEAN STARLING

The common starling, known as the European starling in North America, Is a medium-sized passerine bird with glossy black plumage. About 20 cm (8 in) long, it has pink legs and a black bill in winter, Yellow in summer, with browner plumage in young birds. Noisy in communal roosts, it boasts a varied but unmusical song, And a gift for mimicry noted in literature throughout history. With about 12 subspecies, it breeds across temperate Europe, Migrating south and west in winter, while some reach Iberia and North Africa.
